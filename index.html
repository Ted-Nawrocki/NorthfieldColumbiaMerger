Transaction Analysis
Announced February 2, 2026  •  New Jersey / New York

Two Community Banks. One Highly Structured Transaction.

Columbia Financial's acquisition of Northfield Bancorp is simultaneously a mutual-to-stock conversion, a $1.4–$1.9 billion public offering, and a corporate merger—all sequenced in precise order, each contingent on the last. When complete, the combined institution will rank as the third-largest regional bank headquartered in New Jersey.

Background

A Deal Years in the Making

On February 2, 2026, Columbia Financial, Inc. (Nasdaq: CLBK) and Northfield Bancorp, Inc. (Nasdaq: NFBK) announced a definitive merger agreement under which Columbia will acquire Northfield for approximately $597 million. 8-K, Feb. 2, 2026

Northfield Bank was organized in 1887 as the Northfield Building and Loan Association on the north shore of Staten Island, New York. The bank took its name from the Town of Northfield—one of the four original administrative townships into which Staten Island was divided by the English in 1688. That township was dissolved in 1898 upon consolidation into New York City; its name survives today in the bank itself and in community organizations along the north shore. Over more than 135 years, Northfield grew to become a cornerstone institution on Staten Island, extending its reach into Brooklyn and four New Jersey counties. At announcement, Northfield operated 19 branches on Staten Island alone, representing a deposit base of approximately $2.1 billion in the New York boroughs. NFBK LinkedIn — Wikipedia, Town of Northfield — S-1, Mar. 2026

Columbia Bank was founded in 1927 as a federally chartered savings bank in Fair Lawn, New Jersey. Following its mutual holding company reorganization in 1997 and its minority public offering in 2018, Columbia has executed four acquisitions and grown its balance sheet to more than $10.8 billion in assets. CLBK 10-K, 2025

The origins of this specific transaction trace to a June 13, 2025 meeting between Columbia CEO Thomas Kemly and Northfield CEO Steven Klein, during which Kemly raised the concept of a simultaneous second-step conversion and acquisition. That conversation followed a period in which the Northfield board had explored and ultimately declined a separate transaction with an unnamed third party, whose indication of value ranged from $10.29 to $12.84 per Northfield share. S-4, Mar. 6, 2026

"This merger will strengthen our over 130-year commitment to the people and businesses of Staten Island and provide greater lending capacity to our customers."

Steven M. Klein, Chairman, President and CEO, Northfield Bancorp — Joint Press Release, February 2, 2026

The Northfield board unanimously approved the merger agreement. Directors and executive officers of both companies entered into support agreements committing to vote their shares in favor of the transaction. Settlement agreements were also reached with Northfield's executive officers, providing lump-sum payments totaling over $13 million, contingent on closing. NFBK 8-K, Feb. 2, 2026

The anticipated closing is early in the third quarter of 2026, subject to regulatory approvals, shareholder and depositor votes, completion of the second-step conversion, and other customary conditions. Full systems integration of Northfield Bank into Columbia Bank is targeted for the first quarter of 2027. S-1, Mar. 6, 2026

Post-merger, approximately 19% of the combined company's branches will be located in New York City boroughs—marking Columbia's first entry into the New York market. Banking Dive, Feb. 2026

Transaction Architecture

A Three-Part Transaction in Sequential Order

This is not a conventional bank acquisition. Structures of this kind—a simultaneous second-step conversion and merger—are rare. Each of the three events described below must execute in strict sequence, governed by separate legal documents, separate regulatory approvals, and separate shareholder and depositor votes. A failure or delay at any stage blocks the stages that follow. In aggregate, the transaction generates three distinct corporate actions, each landing on a different shareholder population simultaneously.

1
Step One
Mutual-to-Stock Conversion
Columbia Bank MHC converts from a mutual holding company to a stock holding company. A new Maryland corporation, also named Columbia Financial, Inc., is formed as the post-conversion parent. The MHC and the existing Delaware Columbia Financial entity are both dissolved. The MHC's 73.1% ownership interest is monetized through the public offering in Step 2. S-1, Mar. 2026
2
Step Two
The Conversion Stock Offering
The new Maryland holding company sells shares representing the MHC's former ownership interest at $10.00 per share. The offering proceeds through a defined priority waterfall: eligible Columbia Bank depositors, the ESOP, supplemental depositors, a community offering for residents of eleven New Jersey counties, and a firm-commitment institutional backstop. Gross proceeds range from $1.424B to $1.926B, with a midpoint of $1.675B. S-1, Mar. 2026
3
Step Three
Merger of Northfield Bancorp
Immediately following the close of the conversion and offering, Northfield Bancorp merges into the new Columbia Financial holding company, and Northfield Bank merges into Columbia Bank. Northfield shareholders receive either stock or cash at a ratio determined by the final independent appraisal, subject to a 30% aggregate cash cap. Form 425, Feb. 2026
Corporate Actions Embedded in This Transaction

Three Simultaneous Corporate Actions. Three Different Shareholder Populations.

Corporate actions professionals and securities operations teams should note that this transaction is not a single event processed through one workflow. It embeds three distinct corporate action types, each with different mechanics, different eligible populations, and different operational touchpoints—executing in rapid succession around the same closing date.

Corporate Action 1
Mandatory Share Exchange
Affected Population: Existing CLBK Public Shareholders

Every holder of Columbia Financial (Delaware) common stock will have their shares automatically exchanged for shares of the new Maryland holding company at a ratio of 1.8729x to 2.5340x per current CLBK share, depending on the final independent appraisal. This is a mandatory event—there is no election, no opt-out, and no cash alternative. The Delaware entity ceases to exist. S-1, Mar. 2026

Operations Note
New CUSIP. New issuer. Position records at DTC and at every downstream custodian must be updated at conversion close. The existing CLBK CUSIP will be retired simultaneously with the dissolution of the Delaware entity.
Corporate Action 2
Priority Subscription Offering
Affected Population: Eligible Columbia Bank Depositors

While not a traditional rights offering in the securities law sense, the conversion subscription offering follows the same fundamental logic: a defined population receives a priority entitlement to purchase newly issued shares at a fixed price before the offering opens to the broader public. Eligible depositors as of December 31, 2024 receive subscription rights proportional to their deposit balances. Maximum per subscriber: $500,000 (50,000 shares). S-1, Mar. 2026

Operations Note
The subscription rights carry no CUSIP and are not DTC-eligible. There is no secondary market for the rights. All subscriptions are submitted via paper stock order forms to the Stock Information Center. Orders are irrevocable once submitted.
Corporate Action 3
Merger Consideration Election
Affected Population: Northfield Bancorp Shareholders

Each NFBK shareholder must make a stock or cash election prior to the merger effective date, subject to a 30% aggregate cash cap and proration. The exchange ratio and cash price are not fixed—they float with the final independent appraisal across three valuation tiers. The stock consideration consists of shares in the new Maryland holding company, an issuer with no prior trading history. Merger Agreement, Feb. 2026

Operations Note
NFBK shareholders electing stock will receive shares in an issuer whose reference price at delivery is set by appraisal methodology, not market price discovery. Proration mechanics apply if cash elections exceed the 30% aggregate cap.

Sequencing matters. The mandatory CLBK share exchange and the priority subscription offering execute at conversion close—before the Northfield merger is effective. The NFBK consideration election is a separate event that follows. For custodians, broker-dealers, and transfer agents processing these events, each corporate action requires distinct operational handling despite closing within the same transaction window. S-1, Mar. 2026

Second-Step Conversion & Public Offering

Converting 73.1% of a Bank Into Public Stock

The conversion offering is the largest thrift second-step conversion in the New York-New Jersey metropolitan area in recent memory. At the midpoint, it raises $1.675 billion—nearly four times the size of Columbia's 2018 first-step offering.

Columbia Bank MHC currently owns 76,016,524 shares of Columbia Financial, representing 73.1% of the company. Those shares have never been publicly traded. In the second-step conversion, the MHC's interest is monetized through a public stock offering by the newly formed Maryland holding company at $10.00 per share. The MHC and the existing Delaware Columbia Financial both dissolve upon completion. S-1, Mar. 2026

The offering range, set by an independent appraisal conducted by RP Financial, LC., runs from a minimum of 142,375,000 shares ($1.424 billion) to a maximum of 192,625,000 shares ($1.926 billion). The Federal Reserve Board must provide non-objection to the final appraisal before the offering can proceed. RP Financial will update the appraisal twice: once at the S-1 effective date and again immediately before the company goes to market. RP Financial Appraisal, Ex. 99.3, S-4

Existing CLBK Shareholder Exchange

Existing public holders of Columbia Financial (Delaware) shares will not participate in the cash offering. Their shares will be exchanged for shares of the new Maryland holding company at a ratio intended to preserve their existing percentage ownership interest.

The exchange ratio for existing public CLBK shareholders ranges from 1.8729 to 2.5340 shares of the new holding company per current CLBK share, depending on the final independent valuation. S-1, Mar. 2026

The aggregate merger consideration, based on the midpoint of the conversion offering range and a 1.425x exchange ratio, is approximately $597.1 million. Columbia expects to pay no more than $179.1 million in cash and issue approximately 41,800,140 shares, assuming 70% stock and 30% cash elections. S-1, Mar. 2026

Material Risks & Contingencies

Open Questions and Structural Dependencies

Transactions of this structure are uncommon precisely because their execution risk is multiplicative, not additive. Each of the three sequential events carries its own approval requirements, regulatory gatekeepers, and failure conditions. A problem in any one stage does not merely delay that stage—it blocks every stage that follows. The risks below are drawn from the S-1 and S-4 registration statements filed with the SEC on March 6, 2026.

Sequential Contingency: Three Gates, One Chain
The merger cannot close unless the conversion closes first. The conversion cannot close unless the S-1 is declared effective by the SEC, the Federal Reserve Board provides non-objection to the final appraisal, Columbia Bank depositors vote to approve the conversion plan, and both companies' shareholders approve the merger. Each condition is a hard gate. There is no mechanism to close Step 3 independently if Steps 1 or 2 encounter a delay or failure. This structure has no parallel in standard merger-of-equals transactions. S-1, Mar. 2026
Floating Consideration
The exchange ratio and per-share cash consideration are not fixed at signing. They depend on RP Financial's final appraisal, updated twice before closing. If the final valuation falls materially below the preliminary midpoint, consideration could shift to the lower tier or trigger a renegotiation. S-1, Mar. 2026
Multifamily and Rent-Regulated Exposure
Northfield's loan portfolio is heavily concentrated in multifamily real estate, including New York City rent-regulated units. The aggregate credit and interest rate mark on the rent-regulated portfolio is 14%. Columbia and SRA Consulting reviewed 100% of rent-regulated, non-performing, and classified loans during due diligence. Investor Presentation, Feb. 2026
New York City Market Entry
Post-merger, approximately 19% of combined branches will be located in New York City boroughs, marking Columbia's first entry into the New York market. The S-4 discloses risks related to Columbia's lack of operating history in these markets. S-4, Mar. 2026
Offering Undersubscription
The conversion offering must sell a minimum of 142,375,000 shares to close. If the subscription and community offerings are undersubscribed below the adjusted minimum and the Northfield merger shares cannot bridge the gap, the entire offering would need to be repriced and resolicited. S-1, Mar. 2026
Shareholder Litigation
As of early 2026, Monteverde & Associates PC and Kahn Swick & Foti, LLC each publicly disclosed investigations into the fairness of the merger to Northfield shareholders. No formal complaint had been publicly filed as of the date of this analysis. PR Newswire, Feb. 2, 2026
Integration Complexity
Full systems integration of Northfield Bank into Columbia Bank is targeted for Q1 2027. The transaction involves two distinct regulatory systems, two branch networks, and two organizational cultures. Columbia has completed four prior acquisitions since 2019, providing operational precedent. CLBK 10-K, 2025
First-Year Buyback Restriction
Federal Reserve Board regulations prohibit the new holding company from repurchasing its common stock during the first year following completion of the second-step conversion, limiting capital management flexibility in the initial trading period. S-1, Mar. 2026
